# Designing for iOS Reference

Official source: https://developer.apple.com/design/human-interface-guidelines/designing-for-ios

Use this file when checking whether an app feels at home on iPhone.

## Device and Use Context

- iPhone is a medium-size, high-resolution, highly personal display that people use while moving between places and tasks.
- People commonly hold iPhone in one or both hands, shift between portrait and landscape when useful, and view it from a short distance.
- Multi-Touch, virtual keyboards, voice, motion sensors, location, biometrics, payments, and personal data can improve the experience when permissioned and clearly valuable.
- Sessions vary from quick checks to long media/game/browsing use. The UI should support fast re-entry and frequent app switching.

## iOS Review Checks

- Keep the primary task and content prominent. Limit visible controls, but keep secondary actions discoverable with minimal interaction.
- Adapt cleanly to orientation, Dark Mode, Dynamic Type, and other user-selected configurations.
- Put frequently used controls where they work with common one-handed grips, often near the middle or bottom of the display.
- Preserve familiar navigation gestures where appropriate, including back swipes and row actions.
- Use platform capabilities to reduce manual data entry only when the app has permission and a clear benefit.
- Make system integrations visible and understandable: widgets, Home Screen quick actions, Spotlight, Shortcuts, and activity/share views should match the app's real tasks.

## Evidence to Collect

- Screenshots or simulator checks at a representative small iPhone and large iPhone size.
- Portrait/landscape behavior when the app supports both.
- Dark Mode and large Dynamic Type behavior for the reviewed screens.
- Reachability and one-handed ergonomics for frequent actions.
